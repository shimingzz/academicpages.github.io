My research sits at the intersection of medical imaging, MRI physics, medical signal processing, artificial intelligence, and scientific computing. I am broadly interested in how MR signals are generated, reconstructed, quantified, modeled, and translated into biological or clinical markers.

Medical Imaging and Quantitative MRI
======

I am interested in medical imaging methods that connect acquisition physics, signal formation, quantitative biomarkers, and clinical interpretation. My current work centers on MRI, but the broader research direction includes multimodal imaging, image reconstruction, quantitative mapping, segmentation, quality control, and clinically meaningful feature extraction.

Rather than treating images only as pictures, I think of them as measurements produced by physical systems, acquisition protocols, signal models, reconstruction algorithms, and downstream statistical or AI models.

MRI Signal, Medical Physics, and Microstructure
======

My MRI interests include diffusion MRI, relaxometry, quantitative tissue mapping, MR signal modeling, partial-volume effects, image artifacts, and the physical/statistical assumptions behind derived biomarkers. I am especially interested in how gray matter and white matter microstructure can be modeled from MRI signals and connected to disease mechanisms.

This includes diffusion tensor imaging, NODDI, free-water imaging, fixel-based analysis, tract-level white matter features, cortical microstructure, and broader medical signal processing questions that determine whether a biomarker is reproducible, interpretable, and robust across cohorts.

AI, Computational Imaging, and Scientific Computing
======

I work with AI models as part of the full imaging pipeline, not only as final classifiers. Relevant methods include deep learning, graph neural networks, transformers, self-supervised learning, domain adaptation, representation learning, uncertainty estimation, interpretability, and calibration.

I am also interested in computational imaging, inverse problems, optimization, statistical inference, and reproducible scientific computing. In practice, this means building pipelines that can handle registration, parcellation, tract segmentation, feature extraction, quality control, model training, validation, and reporting without leaking information across analysis stages.

Brain Networks and Translational Neuroimaging
======

Brain-network analysis is one direction within my broader medical imaging interests. I build subject-level brain graphs from MRI-derived feature profiles and study how network topology relates to disease, aging, and cognition. Morphometric similarity networks, microstructure-enriched similarity networks, connectomics, and graph learning are useful tools when the biological signal is distributed across systems rather than localized to a single region.

My dissertation applies this framework to mild cognitive impairment and near-term dementia conversion. It combines multimodal MRI phenotyping, network-level modeling, and domain-aware graph learning across public and local cohorts. The goal is not only prediction, but also a clearer link between MR-derived tissue signals, distributed brain vulnerability, model validity, and future clinical translation.

Current Dissertation Thread
======

My current project develops a microstructure-to-network-to-model framework for dementia conversion in MCI. It includes a systematic review of morphology-derived similarity networks and GNNs, an empirical multimodal MRI phenotype study, a microstructure-enriched brain-network analysis, and a domain-aware graph-learning study using ADNI and local cohort data.
